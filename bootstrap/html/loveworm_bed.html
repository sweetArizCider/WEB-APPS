Produced by Joseph Rogers & Pete Robertson Written by beabadoobee This song is about reflecting on a once-happy relationship that has now lost the love it once had – Bea wonders and wants for the love she and this person used to have that has now ‘disappeared’.

Produced by Pete Robertson & Joseph Rodgers Written by beabadoobee “1999” is the second track off of the “Loveworm” EP. In an interview wi th Billboard, Bea said the song is “about the frustration you feel when you need to let someone go, but y ou can’t because you’re still deeply in love with them.” She later said she really wanted the song to have a “movie soundtrack” sound, which was achieved through synths and cellos along with the help of her producer, Pete.

Produced by Joseph Rodgers & Pete Robertson Written by Joseph Rodgers, Pete Robertson I wanted this song to represent the innocence of love, how a tiny crush can leave someone in denial and a little confused. I wanted a guitar sound that reminded me of Sonic Youth because they’re a band I listen to when I’m in a good mood, and that’s exactly when I wanted people to listen to the song – when they’re happy and just wanna dance. Also, I wanted the melody of the song t o remind me of Pavement, another one of my favourite bands. My best friend Eliana helpe d shout at the end of the track, we get pretty crazy together.

Produced by Joseph Rodgers & Pete Robertson Written by Pete Robertson, Joseph RodgersIn ‘Ceilings’, Bea invites us to envision a kind of relationship that is nurturing, a love that recognises and tends every bit of each other, even the most ‘broken’ parts. She chants about the rightfully boundless nature of life – and that just like it, love, too, should have no ceiling.

Produced by Joseph Rodgers & Pete Robertson Written by Pete Robertson, Joseph Rodgers‘You Lie All the Time’ tells of a relationship shrouded in a fog of doubt, where Bea and her lover mutually tiptoe around serious problems in their relationship. Tired of confrontation and afraid of loss yet being painfully aware of it all, the two practice turning a blind eye (in lying that everything is fine). They play hot potato on the definitive closure of these problems, perpetually leaving loose ends.
